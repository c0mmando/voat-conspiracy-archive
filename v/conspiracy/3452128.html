It depends on the isotope. A lot of the bad ones like Iodine-131 and Strontium-90 are heavy particles and sink to the bottom of the ocean. Its relatively harmless and only affects the organisms that live there. But the really nasty one, Cesium-137 does not sink. A lot of it floats in mid water and attaches to plankton and other aquatic plants. It acts a lot like mercury in that there are tiny amounts in the smaller fish but accumulates in the larger predatory fish that eat them like tuna, mahi-mahi, swordfish. Pretty much all the delicious ones. Cesium-137 is the most deadly and has the longest half-life meaning it sticks around for awhile.

What affect this is having on the oceans? I don't really know because not a lot of studies or truth is really coming out. I remember back in 2012-2013ish there were some warnings on the west coast to eat less pacific bluefin tuna because of potential radiation. Pacific sardine fisheries have collapsed in recent years. I don't have any evidence to show that is caused by radiation though. I have read studies that scientists have found trace amounts of radiation in many fish that were caught around Japan but is harmless to humans. Who knows what the truth really is.
