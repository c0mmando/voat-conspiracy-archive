Everything you write describes exactly how the atmosphere behaves at sea-level. The image is wavy close to the surface, because there is a layer of air convection caused by the temperature difference between the sea and the temperature of the air above it. You can look at any zoom lens photo done at sea-level looking out to the horizon during a warm summer day, and see exactly the same thing.

So everything being wavy and out of focus is exactly how it should look. If it wasn't looking like that, it would be out of the ordinary, as it only happens when the air and sea temperatures match precisely along the line of sight.

The image is in focus at higher altitudes, because the temperature of the air is uniform at higher altitudes.

If, for example, you photograph the moon at zenith, you will always get a better shot than when photographing it at the horizon, because at the horizon you will get atmospheric distortion, just like in this video. Same goes for photographing any far away objects at the horizon. And if the temperature difference is big enough, you can even get a mirage, like this one .
