NoisyCricket 2018-02-27T22:34

As someone who worked on the digital radio system for a specific vendor which is commonly used by law enforcement and various three letter agencies, I can comment on this. No, I won't disclose who I worked for or exactly what I did.

SOP is to change to a secure (encrypted) channel during these types of events - if one is available. Generally this implies a larger department, but not always. BUT... normally this is only done for specific use, such as those inside. Thereby ensuring the bad guys are not actively listening to them clearing the building. Those coordinating with emergency services and other police in the area would still maintain communication on normal, unencrypted channels. This is also true for extra-departmental communication. So for example, Sheriff to police. Though it's possible they may have pre-arranged, encrypted channels, with shared keys. While possible, this is less likely because generally this becomes iffy when different brands are in use because of the control and key/rekeying concerns and maintenance for lost radios.

Also, frequently the press is provided radios in specific markets which are keyed to allow monitoring of certain encrypted channels.

While I am making some assumptions, in general, you should not observe a situation where communication literally goes completely silent. Also, those with scanners should have still picked up encrypted communication (distorted garbage and chirps). Which is what it sounds like when the channel is used but the communication is encrypted. Think something sort of like the old modem handshakes.
